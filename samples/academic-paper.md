# A Randomised Controlled Trial of Early Surgery versus Physiotherapy for Acute Anterior Cruciate Ligament Injuries: Five-Year Follow-up

## Abstract

**Background** — Anterior cruciate ligament (ACL) rupture is a common knee injury among young, physically active adults. Whether early reconstructive surgery or a structured programme of physiotherapy yields superior long-term functional outcomes remains a subject of considerable debate.

**Methods** — We conducted a multicentre, parallel-group, randomised controlled trial across four teaching hospitals in Nigeria, South Africa, and Japan. One hundred and twenty-four adults (aged 18–40 years) with acute, isolated ACL rupture confirmed on magnetic resonance imaging were randomly assigned (1:1) to early ACL reconstruction followed by physiotherapy (surgery group, *n* = 62) or to a structured, progressive physiotherapy programme with optional delayed reconstruction if instability persisted (physiotherapy group, *n* = 62). The primary outcome was the Knee Injury and Osteoarthritis Outcome Score (KOOS) at five years. Secondary outcomes included the Tegner activity scale, return to pre-injury sport, the incidence of subsequent meniscal surgery, and radiographic evidence of tibiofemoral osteoarthritis. Analysis was by intention to treat.

**Findings** — At five years, 117 participants (94%) completed follow-up. The mean KOOS scores did not differ significantly between the surgery and physiotherapy groups (mean difference 1.2 points, 95% CI −3.4 to 5.8; *p* = 0.61). Thirty-eight percent of participants in the physiotherapy group underwent delayed ACL reconstruction within the five-year window. There was no significant difference in the proportion of participants who developed radiographic osteoarthritis (22% surgery versus 19% physiotherapy; risk ratio 1.16, 95% CI 0.61–2.20; *p* = 0.65). However, the physiotherapy group experienced a higher rate of subsequent meniscal surgery (18% versus 5%; *p* = 0.03).

**Interpretation** — In young, active adults with acute ACL rupture, a strategy of initial physiotherapy with optional delayed reconstruction yields functional outcomes equivalent to early surgery at five years but carries an increased risk of subsequent meniscal injury. These findings support a shared decision-making approach in which both strategies are presented as reasonable initial management options.

## Introduction

Anterior cruciate ligament rupture is among the most common serious knee injuries sustained during sporting activities, with an estimated annual incidence of 68.6 per 100,000 person-years in the general population and considerably higher rates among participants in pivoting sports such as football, basketball, and netball [1,2]. The injury typically occurs through a non-contact mechanism involving sudden deceleration combined with a change of direction or landing from a jump, producing the characteristic "pop" followed by acute haemarthrosis [3].

The management of ACL rupture has evolved considerably over the past three decades. Historically, the injury was treated with open primary repair, a technique largely abandoned because of unacceptably high failure rates [4]. The modern era has been dominated by arthroscopic ACL reconstruction using autograft tissue, most commonly bone–patellar tendon–bone or quadrupled semitendinosus/gracilis grafts [5]. In parallel, structured rehabilitation programmes have become increasingly sophisticated, moving from simple strengthening protocols to integrated programmes that address neuromuscular control, proprioceptive deficits, and psychological readiness to return to sport [6,7].

Despite the volume of research, fundamental questions persist. First, does early ACL reconstruction confer a clinically meaningful benefit over a well-structured programme of physiotherapy with optional delayed reconstruction? Second, does either strategy alter the natural history of post-traumatic osteoarthritis, which develops in a substantial proportion of patients irrespective of treatment? Third, are there identifiable subgroups — defined by age, activity level, associated injuries, or biomechanical profile — for whom one strategy is clearly preferable?

Previous randomised trials have produced conflicting results. Frobell and colleagues reported no significant difference in patient-reported outcomes at two and five years between early reconstruction and optional delayed reconstruction in the KANON trial, although the rate of subsequent meniscal surgery was higher in the physiotherapy-first group [8,9]. In contrast, a meta-analysis by Monk et al. suggested that ACL reconstruction resulted in higher rates of return to sport but also a higher prevalence of radiographic osteoarthritis [10]. Methodological limitations of the existing literature include small sample sizes in many trials, heterogeneity of outcome measures, and relatively short follow-up that may not capture the full trajectory of post-traumatic osteoarthritis.

We therefore designed the ACL Treatment Evaluation Study (ACL-TES) to compare the five-year functional and radiographic outcomes of early ACL reconstruction versus a structured physiotherapy programme with optional delayed reconstruction in young, physically active adults with acute, isolated ACL rupture.

## Methods

### Study Design and Oversight

ACL-TES was an investigator-initiated, multicentre, parallel-group, randomised controlled trial conducted at four teaching hospitals in Nigeria (University of Lagos Teaching Hospital and University of Ibadan Teaching Hospital), South Africa (Tygerberg Hospital, Stellenbosch), and Japan (Kyoto University Hospital). The trial protocol has been published previously [11] and is available with the full text of this article. The study was approved by the institutional review board at each participating centre, and all participants provided written informed consent prior to enrolment.

An independent data and safety monitoring board reviewed accumulating data at annual intervals. The trial was supervised by an international steering committee that included orthopaedic surgeons, physiotherapists, biostatisticians, a patient representative, and an independent chair. All authors vouch for the accuracy and completeness of the data and for the fidelity of the trial to the protocol.

### Participants

Eligible participants were adults aged 18 to 40 years who had sustained an acute (within four weeks of enrolment), isolated ACL rupture confirmed on magnetic resonance imaging (MRI). Key exclusion criteria were: concomitant complete rupture of the medial collateral ligament (grade III), posterior cruciate ligament injury, or posterolateral corner injury; a history of previous knee surgery on the index limb; radiographic evidence of moderate or severe osteoarthritis (Kellgren–Lawrence grade ≥3) at baseline; bilateral knee injuries; pregnancy; and any medical condition that would contraindicate surgery or intensive physiotherapy.

A total of 324 patients were screened for eligibility between 1 March 2020 and 31 August 2021. Of these, 124 underwent randomisation (see Figure 1 for the CONSORT flow diagram).

### Randomisation and Masking

Participants were randomly assigned in a 1:1 ratio to either early ACL reconstruction (surgery group) or structured physiotherapy with optional delayed reconstruction (physiotherapy group). Randomisation was performed centrally through a secure, web-based system using a computer-generated sequence with permuted blocks of variable size (4, 6, or 8), stratified by centre, sex, and age group (18–28 years versus 29–40 years). Allocation concealment was ensured by the centralised system, which released the treatment assignment only after the participant's baseline data had been entered and verified.

Because of the nature of the interventions, blinding of participants and treating clinicians was not feasible. However, outcome assessors — including the radiologists who evaluated follow-up radiographs and the physiotherapists who administered the functional tests — were masked to treatment allocation. The trial biostatistician remained blinded until the primary analysis was complete.

### Interventions

#### Surgery Group

Participants assigned to the surgery group underwent arthroscopic single-bundle ACL reconstruction within eight weeks of randomisation. The surgical technique was standardised across centres. All procedures used an ipsilateral quadrupled semitendinosus/gracilis autograft. Femoral tunnel placement was performed via an anteromedial portal, and fixation was achieved with an adjustable-loop cortical button on the femur and a bioabsorbable interference screw with supplementary staple fixation on the tibia. Associated meniscal injuries were addressed at the time of surgery: unstable meniscal tears were repaired where possible; otherwise, a minimal partial meniscectomy was performed. Post-operatively, all participants followed an identical, protocol-driven rehabilitation programme (see Supplementary Appendix).

#### Physiotherapy Group

Participants in the physiotherapy group commenced a structured, progressive, supervised physiotherapy programme within one week of randomisation. The programme was delivered by experienced musculoskeletal physiotherapists at each centre and consisted of three phases. Phase 1 (weeks 1–6) focused on reducing effusion, restoring range of motion, and initiating quadriceps and hamstring activation. Phase 2 (weeks 7–16) introduced progressive strengthening, balance and proprioceptive training, and controlled agility drills. Phase 3 (weeks 17–24) involved sport-specific rehabilitation, plyometric training, and a structured return-to-sport assessment. Participants who experienced persistent, functionally limiting knee instability despite completing at least 16 weeks of physiotherapy were offered delayed ACL reconstruction; the decision was made jointly by the participant, the treating surgeon, and the physiotherapist.

#### Common Elements

All participants, regardless of group assignment, received a standardised information booklet and were asked to refrain from contact and pivoting sports for a minimum of six months. No intra-articular corticosteroid injections or viscosupplementation were permitted during the follow-up period. The use of oral non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs was permitted as needed.

### Outcome Measures

The primary outcome was the Knee Injury and Osteoarthritis Outcome Score (KOOS) at five years. The KOOS is a validated, 42-item, patient-reported questionnaire that assesses five domains: Pain, Symptoms, Function in Daily Living, Function in Sport and Recreation, and Knee-Related Quality of Life. Scores range from 0 (extreme problems) to 100 (no problems) [12].

Prespecified secondary outcomes included: the Tegner activity scale [13]; the proportion of participants who returned to their pre-injury level of sport; the incidence of subsequent meniscal surgery (either primary surgery in the physiotherapy group or re-operation in the surgery group); the proportion of participants who crossed over to delayed reconstruction in the physiotherapy group; the incidence of graft failure or revision ACL surgery in the surgery group; radiographic tibiofemoral osteoarthritis assessed using the Kellgren–Lawrence classification (grade ≥2 considered positive) [14] on weight-bearing anteroposterior and lateral radiographs obtained at the five-year visit; and health-related quality of life assessed with the EQ-5D-5L [15].

Adverse events were recorded throughout the five-year follow-up period and classified as serious (resulting in death, disability, or prolonged hospitalisation) or non-serious. All events were adjudicated by an independent clinical events committee masked to treatment assignment.

### Statistical Analysis

The sample size calculation was based on detecting a between-group difference of 8 points in the KOOS score at five years (the minimal clinically important difference for the KOOS in ACL-injured populations [16]), with an assumed standard deviation of 18 points, 80% power, and a two-sided alpha of 0.05. This yielded a requirement of 52 participants per group. To account for an anticipated 15% loss to follow-up, we planned to enrol 124 participants.

The primary analysis was performed on an intention-to-treat basis, with all participants analysed according to their randomly assigned group regardless of the treatment actually received. We used a linear mixed-effects model for the primary outcome, with treatment group, time (as a categorical variable), the treatment-by-time interaction, and the stratification factors (centre, sex, and age group) as fixed effects, and participant-specific random intercepts. The model included all available data under the missing-at-random assumption. As a sensitivity analysis, we used multiple imputation by chained equations (50 imputed datasets) to assess the robustness of the findings to departures from the missing-at-random assumption.

Binary secondary outcomes were analysed using log-binomial regression (or modified Poisson regression with robust standard errors when the log-binomial model failed to converge), with results expressed as risk ratios. Time-to-event outcomes (delayed reconstruction, graft failure, meniscal surgery) were analysed using the Kaplan–Meier method and compared with the log-rank test; hazard ratios were estimated from Cox proportional-hazards models.

All analyses were conducted with R version 4.3.2 (R Foundation for Statistical Computing, Vienna, Austria). A two-sided *p* value of less than 0.05 was considered statistically significant, with no adjustment for multiplicity; therefore, secondary outcome analyses should be considered exploratory.

### Primary Outcome

At five years, 117 of 124 participants (94%) completed the KOOS questionnaire. The unadjusted mean KOOS score was 78.4 (95% CI 74.1 to 82.7) in the surgery group and 77.2 (95% CI 72.9 to 81.5) in the physiotherapy group. In the primary mixed-effects model, the between-group difference was 1.2 points (95% CI −3.4 to 5.8; *p* = 0.61), which did not reach statistical significance and was well below the prespecified minimal clinically important difference of 8 points. The results were consistent across all prespecified sensitivity analyses, including multiple imputation (adjusted mean difference 0.9 points; 95% CI −3.8 to 5.6; *p* = 0.71) and per-protocol analysis restricted to participants who adhered to their assigned treatment (adjusted mean difference 0.4 points; 95% CI −5.1 to 5.9; *p* = 0.89).

Analysis of the individual KOOS subscales revealed a consistent pattern of no significant between-group differences, with the possible exception of the Sport and Recreation subscale, in which the surgery group scored marginally higher (mean difference 6.0 points; 95% CI 0.3 to 11.7; nominal *p* = 0.04).

At five years, 38% of participants assigned to the physiotherapy group had undergone delayed ACL reconstruction. The median time to delayed reconstruction was 14 months (interquartile range 10–23). The cumulative incidence curves for delayed reconstruction are shown in Figure 2 (see Supplementary Appendix).

The incidence of subsequent meniscal surgery was significantly higher in the physiotherapy group than in the surgery group (18% versus 5%; risk ratio 0.27, 95% CI 0.08–0.91; *p* = 0.03). The majority of these procedures were performed in conjunction with delayed ACL reconstruction and involved the medial meniscus (13 of 14 cases). No participant in either group underwent total knee arthroplasty during the follow-up period.

### Adverse Events

During the five-year follow-up period, 11 serious adverse events were recorded: 6 in the surgery group and 5 in the physiotherapy group (Table 3). In the surgery group, serious adverse events comprised 2 cases of deep surgical site infection requiring arthroscopic lavage and intravenous antibiotics, 1 case of symptomatic deep vein thrombosis, 1 case of arthrofibrosis requiring manipulation under anaesthesia, and 2 graft failures requiring revision reconstruction. In the physiotherapy group, serious adverse events comprised 3 knee injuries sustained during sports participation (one of which was a contralateral ACL rupture), 1 case of complex regional pain syndrome, and 1 case of septic arthritis following a corticosteroid injection performed outside the trial protocol.

## Discussion

### Principal Findings

In this multicentre, randomised trial involving young, physically active adults with acute ACL rupture, we found no significant difference in the primary outcome — the KOOS score at five years — between participants assigned to early ACL reconstruction and those assigned to a structured physiotherapy programme with optional delayed reconstruction. The point estimate of the between-group difference (1.2 points) was far below the prespecified minimal clinically important difference of 8 points, and the 95% confidence interval excluded a clinically meaningful benefit for either strategy. These results are consistent with the findings of the KANON trial [8,9] and extend them to a more diverse, predominantly African population with a longer follow-up period.

However, the physiotherapy-first strategy was associated with a significantly higher rate of subsequent meniscal surgery (18% versus 5%). This finding is also consistent with previous reports [8,9,17] and raises an important question: if functional outcomes are equivalent at five years, does the excess of meniscal procedures in the physiotherapy group represent a clinically important harm, or does it reflect a lower threshold for surgical intervention in patients who have not yet undergone reconstruction? The observation that most meniscal surgeries were performed at the time of delayed ACL reconstruction suggests the latter, at least in part. Nevertheless, meniscal tissue loss is irreversible, and the long-term consequences — particularly with respect to the development of osteoarthritis beyond five years — warrant continued surveillance of this cohort.

### Comparison with Previous Literature

Our findings align with a growing body of evidence that challenges the long-held assumption that ACL reconstruction is the default treatment for all acute ruptures in active individuals. The KANON trial, conducted in Sweden, randomised 121 young, active adults and found no significant difference in the KOOS score at two or five years between early reconstruction and optional delayed reconstruction, although 51% of the physiotherapy group underwent delayed reconstruction [8,9]. The COMPARE trial in the Netherlands similarly reported equivalent functional outcomes at two years [18].

Several systematic reviews and meta-analyses have synthesised this evidence. Monk et al. pooled data from three randomised trials (including KANON) and found no significant difference in patient-reported outcomes but noted a higher rate of return to sport after early reconstruction [10]. A network meta-analysis by Muaidi et al. suggested that surgical reconstruction may confer a modest advantage in knee stability (as measured by instrumented laxity testing) but not in patient-reported function [19]. Our results are consistent with these syntheses and add to the evidence base by providing data from an African population, which has been underrepresented in ACL research to date [20].

### Strengths and Limitations

This trial has several strengths. First, the multicentre, international design enhances the generalisability of the findings across diverse healthcare settings, from high-resource environments (Japan) to settings with more limited surgical capacity (Nigeria). Second, the high rate of follow-up (94% at five years) minimises attrition bias. Third, the use of a centralised, stratified randomisation system ensured allocation concealment and balanced baseline characteristics. Fourth, our analytic approach — a mixed-effects model that uses all available data under the missing-at-random assumption — is efficient and appropriate for longitudinal data.

This trial also has limitations. First, the sample size, although adequate for the primary outcome, provided limited power for subgroup analyses and for the detection of modest differences in secondary outcomes. Second, the inability to blind participants and treating clinicians introduces the possibility of performance and detection biases, although the use of masked outcome assessors mitigates this concern. Third, the trial was conducted in four academic centres with experienced surgeons and physiotherapists; the results may not generalise to settings with less expertise. Fourth, while a five-year follow-up is among the longest reported in ACL trials, it remains insufficient to capture the full trajectory of post-traumatic osteoarthritis, which develops insidiously over decades.

### Clinical Implications

Our findings support a shared decision-making approach to the management of acute ACL rupture. Both early reconstruction and a structured physiotherapy programme with optional delayed reconstruction are reasonable initial strategies, and the choice between them should be guided by patient preferences, activity goals, and local surgical and rehabilitation capacity.

For patients who prioritise the avoidance of surgery and are willing to accept a moderately increased risk of subsequent meniscal injury, a physiotherapy-first approach is supported by the data. For those who prioritise the earliest possible return to high-level pivoting sports and wish to minimise the risk of secondary meniscal damage, early reconstruction remains an appropriate option. Importantly, patients should be informed that, irrespective of the initial strategy chosen, a substantial proportion — approximately one in five — will develop radiographic evidence of osteoarthritis within five years, and this proportion is likely to increase with longer follow-up [21].

### Unanswered Questions and Future Research

Several questions remain. First, are there identifiable subgroups — defined by age, sex, baseline activity level, the presence of associated meniscal injury, or biomechanical factors such as knee hyperextension or tibial slope — for whom one strategy is clearly superior? Our trial was not powered for subgroup analyses, but individual participant data meta-analyses pooling data from ACL-TES, KANON, COMPARE, and other completed trials could address this question. Second, does the excess of meniscal surgery in the physiotherapy-first group translate into a higher incidence of osteoarthritis at ten or twenty years? Extended follow-up of this cohort is planned. Third, can neuromuscular training programmes, bracing, or other conservative interventions further reduce the rate of delayed reconstruction and meniscal injury in patients managed without early surgery?

## Conclusion

In young, active adults with acute ACL rupture, a strategy of structured physiotherapy with optional delayed reconstruction produces functional outcomes equivalent to early surgery at five years. Both strategies are associated with a similar, substantial risk of developing radiographic osteoarthritis. The choice of initial management should be individualised through a shared decision-making process that takes into account the patient's preferences, activity goals, and tolerance for the risk of subsequent meniscal injury.
